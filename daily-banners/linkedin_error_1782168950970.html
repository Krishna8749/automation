Why does marketing feel like it’s not working?


Most of the time, that’s not the real problem

(Credit: Pradeep Kumar)

If the budget feels like buying a cycle
the plan is half-baked,
and expectations are Mustang

fast results will always feel missing.

Then comes the question 
why is growth slow?

Because marketing isn’t just ads.
If positioning isn’t clear, the product isn’t strong, execution isn’t consistent, and the market isn’t understood… results will lag

And one simple truth
marketing can bring customers, but it can’t make them stay

That depends on the product.

Marketing can drive the first purchase.
But if the experience doesn’t match the promise,
customers don’t come back

So it feels like marketing isn’t working…
when it’s actually showing where things are breaking

Get those right, and marketing starts working.

FOLLOW YOUR PASSION
Is the Most Misleading Advice I Was Ever Given

🕯️THE QUESTION
I was asked this question many times:
❔" What are you passionate about? "

At School.
At University.
By people who genuinely wanted to help.

💭 I didn't have an answer.

Not a clear one.
Not something I could say with certainty.
So I did something else.
I worked.
I trained.
I competed.
I studied.

Not because I had found my "Calling"...
→ but because I chose to carry what was in front of me... well.

And over time...
something unexpected happened.

What I carried well...
→ began to matter.

🕯️ THE ASSUMPTION
"Follow your passion" sounds right.

But it quietly assumes:
✓ that passion is clear.
✓ that it exists early.
✓ that it leads.
✓ that it sustains.

But life rarely unfolds that way.

🕯️ THE REALITY
Passion is fragile.
It changes.
It fades.

Skill is built through:
→ Repetition.
→ Effort.
→ Staying... when it's boring.

Humans change.
What you care about at 20...
is not what you carry at 40.

🕯️ THE SHIFT
So the question changes.

Not:
❔" What am I passionate about? "

But:
❔" What am I able to carry... well? "

Because responsibility does something different.
It:
✓ builds skill.
✓ forms character.
✓ reveals limits.
✓ creates consequence.

And slowly...
→ meaning begins to appear.

🕯️ THE TRUTH
Meaning does not come first.
It follows responsibility... carried well.

Not immediately.
Not loudly.
But steadily...
through what you continue to carry.

🕯️ THE RISK
When people wait for passion:
✓ they hesitate.
✓ they drift.
✓ they doubt themselves.

And eventually...
→ they believe something is wrong.

But nothing is wrong...
→ they are simply waiting for something that comes later.

🛞 THE VITRUVIAN WHEEL
Offers a different way of seeing:
→ Life is not a ladder... It turns.
→ Life is not lived in one dimension.
→ Life is carried across four arcs:

Health
Character
Relationships
Meaning

Not sequentially... simultaneously.
Not in parts... but as a whole.

So, when someone says:
→ "Follow your passion"... they are often focusing on one arc...
Meaning.

But ignoring and leaving the others behind.
And that is where imbalance begins...

Because the Wheel does not allow one arc...
to compensate for another.

📜 Long-Reflection
I wrote a long-form reflection called The Vitruvian Wheel,
to offer a different way of seeing... not advice, not instruction,
but orientation.
( see comment on how to download the full text. )

🕯️ THE PRACTICAL QUESTION
So instead of asking:
❔ "What is my passion?"

Ask:
❔ "What is worth carrying... given who I am becoming?"

And then:
→ "Carry it... well enough... long enough... until something changes."

🕯️ CLOSING
Because over time...
what you carry well...
becomes what you care about.

Not the other way around.

Demetris 🪶
🌿🌿🌿 I live always learning.

    🚀 AI Strategy Signals – Vol. 64 (Friday, June 19, 2026) "Vertical AI's Moat Gets Contested"

    Your ongoing twice-weekly briefing cutting through the noise to surface the signals shaping enterprise AI — from models and infrastructure to multi-agent systems, robotics, and deployment realities.

    📍 As we highlighted in Tuesday's signals, vertical, industry-specific AI is no longer a thesis—it's where value and money now land, proven across healthcare, legal, finance, manufacturing, and defense at once. This week, that moat is already contested: incumbents bundle agents into software you own, capital narrows onto a few winners, and in regulated industries governance decides who deploys and who stalls.

    📡 This Week's Signals
    1️⃣ Legal isn't winner-take-all. Legora hit ~$100M ARR at $5.6B; Harvey ~$200M ARR at $11B. Yet firms run both, swapping seats by practice area.
    Why it matters: Even inside one profession, depth wins workflows → not whole markets.

    2️⃣ Agents go from minutes to hours. JPMorgan's new agents run unattended for hours—450+ use cases, $18B budget, private-banking sales up 20%.
    Why it matters: The frontier isn't a smarter model → it's how long agents run unsupervised.

    3️⃣ Incumbents strike back. Gartner: AI is mostly sold by your existing vendor; 2026 AI spend hits $2.52T, up 44%. Agentforce, Copilot Studio, and SAP Joule bundle agents in.
    Why it matters: Build-vs-buy is real → buy common workflows, build what your data makes unique.

    4️⃣ Capital is voting vertical. The AI agents market tops $10.9B, up 45%, with vertical players taking ~72% of funding. Avoca raised $125M for the trades; AI is ~75% of health-tech funding.
    Why it matters: Fewer, bigger bets → investors buy domain ownership, not novelty.

    5️⃣ Governance is the next chokepoint. Just 21% of firms have mature agent governance; four in five with agents in production lack clear accountability. The European Union's AI Act hits general application August 2; Colorado's lands June 30.
    Why it matters: In regulated verticals, governance isn't a tax → it's the moat.

    📈 Trendline
    Tuesday: vertical AI is where value concentrates.
    This week: that value is contested on three fronts—
    1️⃣ incumbents bundling agents into platforms you own
    2️⃣ capital narrowing onto a few domain winners
    3️⃣ governance dividing durable from fragile verticals

    The strategic shift:
    👉 Vertical AI is moving from a land grab → a defensibility contest

    For enterprises:
    The question is no longer whether to go vertical.
    It's whether your moat—domain data, agent autonomy, governance—is something a vendor can bundle and sell your rival.

    After More Than 20 Years in IT, One Question from a Fresher Changed My Perspective

    A few years ago, after conducting a technical training session, a young graduate approached me with a simple question:

    “Sir, what should I focus on to become successful in IT?”

    At first, I was ready to give the usual answer—learn programming, improve problem-solving, understand databases, and keep up with new technologies.

    But then I paused.

    After spending more than two decades in the industry and training over 10,000 professionals, I realized that the people who grow the fastest aren’t always the smartest developers in the room.

    They’re the ones who consistently do a few things differently.

    Here are the lessons I’ve learned throughout my journey:

    ✅ Never stop learning. Technology rewards curiosity.

    ✅ Build strong fundamentals. Trends come and go, but fundamentals stay with you for life.

    ✅ Learn how to communicate. The ability to explain your ideas is often more valuable than knowing every answer.

    ✅ Focus on solving problems, not just writing code.

    ✅ Don’t collect certificates—build projects and gain real experience.

    ✅ Learn how to work with AI. It’s becoming a powerful partner for developers, not a replacement.

    ✅ Be consistent. Small daily improvements create massive long-term results.

    ✅ Collaborate with others. Great careers are built by great teams.

    ✅ Protect your reputation. People remember reliability far longer than technical brilliance.

    ✅ Help others grow. The more people you support, the more opportunities come back to you.

    Looking back, the biggest lesson isn’t about technology at all.

    It’s this:

    Your career is a marathon, not a sprint.

    The professionals who succeed over the long term are not necessarily the most talented. They are the ones who remain humble enough to learn, disciplined enough to improve, and resilient enough to adapt.

    After all these years, I’m still learning every day.

    And that’s probably the reason I’m still enjoying this journey.

    💬 What’s one career lesson you’ve learned that you wish someone had told you earlier?
