It was a privilege to be part of the Lumbini Police Hackathon and present our solution, Traffic Eye.

My team, Shail Malla and Rohan Khanal, was invited to showcase our software to police officials in Lumbini. Meeting so many officers who protect our nation every day was truly inspiring.

This event was extra special for me. I got to reconnect with my mentor, Mr. Monil A., who taught me so much about cybersecurity. I also met Saroj Lamichhane, a pioneer working on a similar project for the police. Hoping to collaborate with you soon, sir, and help strengthen the nation's IT infrastructure.

The problem we are solving:

1. Nepal's officer to citizen ratio is 1:542, meaning one officer handles the queries and problems of 542 people
2. Information is scattered everywhere with no proper layer for searchability
3. The real gap is not infrastructure, it is the missing Actionable Data Layer on top of what already exists
4. Most CCTV footage is stored locally and wiped every few days, leaving valuable data completely unused

Our solution, Traffic Eye:

1. Integrates with existing CCTV cameras using the RTSP protocol and each camera's private IP
2. Uses a custom trained AI/ML model to detect helmet violations
3. Sends real time alerts straight to email
4. Detects traffic congestion
5. Captures, crops and stores license plates, which can be fed back into the system for continuously improving accuracy

Challenges along the way:

Training the computer vision model nearly pushed my PC to its limit. Nepali license plates come in different colors, red, blue, green and black, and each color carries its own meaning, which made the OCR part very tricky.

We tried EasyOCR, PaddleOCR and Tesseract, but none could read the unique font strokes used on Nepali plates. We then built a HOG plus SVM model based on Ashok Panta's research paper. It could recognize numbers and the "Ba" and "Pa" characters well, but struggled to consistently read full plates from live MP4 streams.

Potential impact:

1. Instant review of footage instead of days of manual work, saving time and resources
2. Faster lost vehicle detection by narrowing the search to specific camera paths
3. Better hit and run investigations by tracing the route through detected license plates
4. Smarter automation, where cameras flag congestion or collisions and call in officers only when truly needed

So many people at the event asked how we built this and how they could help. I would love to collaborate further and guide anyone interested in this space. We believe Traffic Eye can make a real difference and even help save lives.

Jay Nepal.

        Everyone wants to build the next big startup. Very few are willing to solve problems that already exist.

        I recently came across the story of a young entrepreneur from Bihar who chose agriculture when many people around him considered it an outdated career path.

        What caught my attention wasn't the revenue figure.

        It was the mindset.

        While others were chasing trends, he focused on a sector that feeds millions of people every day.

        Today, that decision has reportedly grown into a multi-crore agribusiness that works closely with farmers and creates value across the agricultural ecosystem.

        Here are 3 business lessons every entrepreneur can learn from this:

        ✅ Opportunity exists where attention doesn't.
        The most profitable businesses are often built in industries that most people ignore.

        ✅ Solve real problems, and growth follows.
        Businesses scale when they create genuine value, not when they simply generate noise.

        ✅ Respect the fundamentals.
        Agriculture may be one of the oldest industries in the world, but innovation, execution, and trust can still create extraordinary outcomes.

        As founders and business leaders, we often spend too much time searching for the "next big thing."

        Sometimes the biggest opportunity is right in front of us.

        The question is:

        Are we willing to build where others refuse to look?

        Because success doesn't always come from creating something new.

        Sometimes it comes from improving something essential.

        _ Irfan Ansari
